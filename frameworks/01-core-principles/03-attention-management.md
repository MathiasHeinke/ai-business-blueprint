# Attention Management

> *"If you actually just improved one part of your business, everything else will be forced to lift with it."* — Sam Shvatsa

## The principle

You don't have too little time. You have too many priorities. Top performers focus on **2–3 things, maximum**. Everything else is headwind.

## The 80% rule

Spend 80% of your active time on your top 3 levers. Most of that on #1.

Revalidate every quarter:
- What are the 2–3 highest-leverage areas right now?
- What initiatives don't move the needle?
- If you could only do one of them — which?

## The "bet on the right horse" question

Founders often feel like they're guessing which initiative will pay off. You don't have to guess. You can diagnose.

Method: assume an area is solved. Then ask whether everything else still works. → automatically reveals the right priority. (See `../02-diagnostics/03-bottleneck-finder.md`.)

## How to look at it

Attention is scarcer than money. You can earn money back. You can't reclaim attention.

Every "yes" to something new is a "no" to your top priority.

## How to apply it

**Weekly ritual (Sunday, 30 min):**
1. What's the *one* thing this week that would make the biggest difference?
2. Which three activities support that thing?
3. What am I deliberately sacrificing this week to make room?

**Quarterly audit:**
- List every active initiative.
- For each: leverage × energy cost.
- Top 3 reinforce, the rest pause or kill.

## Concrete actions

- [ ] Name your top 3 for this quarter, explicitly.
- [ ] Place them visibly in your workspace.
- [ ] Give every other initiative a status: pause / cut / defer.
- [ ] Establish a Sunday ritual.

## Warning: the curse trap

Smart founders default to *adding* rather than *focusing*. If you feel the urge to "stack one more thing," stop. Read `01-curse-of-capability.md`.
