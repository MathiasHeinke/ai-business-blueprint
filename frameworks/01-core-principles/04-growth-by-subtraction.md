# Growth by Subtraction

> *"You want growth by subtraction, not multiplication."* — Sam Shvatsa

## The principle

Most founders equate growth with addition: more products, more channels, more team, more tasks. From an investor's seat, the opposite is often true: cuts followed by observation — *"You got 90% of your time back and only lost 10% of revenue."*

## How to look at it

Subtraction is a **growth strategy**, not a panic move. Every cut returns attention, energy, and clarity — the raw materials of the next level.

## The math of subtraction

| What you cut | What you gain |
|---|---|
| A service line at 8% revenue, 25% effort | 17 points of attention |
| A channel at 5% leads, 30% operational drag | 25 points of energy |
| A customer at 10% revenue, 50% stress | Mental bandwidth |

## How to apply it

**Build a quarterly cut list:**

1. **Low margin / high effort** — service lines that cost more than they bring.
2. **Strategic misfit** — anything that doesn't reinforce your one value proposition.
3. **Energy drains** — customers, projects, or tools that cost life force.
4. **Legacy structures** — processes that exist because they always have.

**The cut question:** *If we removed this tomorrow — what actually breaks? (And what just feels like it would?)*

## Real example: Telos

The cut filter at Telos was: *Does this help our agents save one day a week?* Anything that didn't help fell away — regardless of how good the idea sounded. Result: 10x in 5 years.

## Concrete actions

- [ ] Sort current activities into four columns: Keep / Improve / Delegate / Cut.
- [ ] Make at least 3 cuts per quarter.
- [ ] After each cut: measure for 30 days — what actually happened?
- [ ] Document the lesson: what really breaks vs. what only feels like it would.

## Why it's hard

Cuts feel like loss. Most of the time they're not — they're focus. Read `01-curse-of-capability.md` for context: precisely because you *can* do it, cutting is hard.
