# Curse of Capability

> *"Smart, capable entrepreneurs put themselves in situations to do complex things — because they're capable of doing those things. I love investing in really lazy entrepreneurs because they're like: this happens, this happens, and I make a lot of money."* — Sam Shvatsa

## The principle

The more capable you are, the more likely you are to build a complex business — because you can. That's the trap. Complexity is not linear, it's **exponential**. A $100M business is not 100x more complex than a $1M business — it's much, much more.

## How to look at it

Every time you add a new service line, a new channel, a new workflow, the question isn't *"Can we do this?"* It's *"What does this cost us in attention, energy, and scalability?"*

Capability is a precondition, not a justification.

## How to apply it

**Audit:** Which service lines, products, or initiatives are you running because you *can*, not because they have the highest leverage?

**Cut test:** Which 30% of your activities consume 70% of your energy and deliver less than 20% of your output?

**Lazy entrepreneur question:** *"What's the laziest possible version of this business that still delivers the result?"* If the answer looks radically different from what you do today, that's a signal.

## Concrete actions

- [ ] List every active service / product line.
- [ ] For each: % of revenue, % of effort, scalability score (1–10).
- [ ] Flag three candidates you keep *because you can*, not because they're strategic.
- [ ] Decide: cut, simplify, or explicitly accept as a cash cow.

## Why this matters

The pattern repeats: an agency offers editing, producing, social, graphic design, web, and VFX — because the team is capable of all of it. Each capability becomes a service line, every service line a department-in-waiting, and the founder ends up running six mini-companies under one roof.

It works. But it doesn't scale. And the longer it runs, the harder it is to undo.

The earlier you decide what *one* thing you do better than anyone else, the easier the next decade is.
