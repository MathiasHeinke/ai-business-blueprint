# Second Order Thinking

> *"While every other founder was asking first-order questions, [the operators I respected] were asking: if I did this, what would it mean for one year, two year, five years, ten years down the line?"* — Sam Shvatsa

## The principle

First order: *"If I do X, what happens immediately?"*
Second order: *"And then what? And then what? And then what?"*

Most decisions don't fail at order 1. They fail at order 2 or 3 — the consequences of consequences.

## How to look at it

Before any major decision, don't only ask *"What does this bring next month?"* Ask *"What does this mean for our setup in 1, 3, 5 years?"*

This prevents short-term wins from boxing you into long-term dead ends.

## The three-horizon question

Score every initiative on three timeframes:

| Horizon | Question |
|---|---|
| **1 year** | Does this generate measurable cash / growth? |
| **3 years** | Does this support a scalable architecture? |
| **5–10 years** | Does this increase or decrease our optionality? |

All three "yes" → green light.
"Yes" on 1, "no" on 3 or 5 → red flag, rethink.

## Don Shula analogy

Legendary NFL coach Don Shula structured every play so that *each one* could result in a touchdown. Not "8 yards is fine" — every play architected to make a touchdown possible.

Translation: choose activities that in the best case have a *large* outcome — not ones that in the best case help a little.

## How to apply it

**The "what if this works?" exercise:**

For every initiative, play out the success case:
- *If this scales to $10M — does the model still work?*
- *If this scales to $100M — what breaks?*

Often you'll see at question 2 that the current model can't hold it. → You need to build differently *today*.

## Concrete actions

- [ ] Run every new initiative through the three-horizon question.
- [ ] On red flags: write a memo (see `../07-communication-and-marketing/01-memo-culture-wafam.md`).
- [ ] Quarterly: stress-test top initiatives against the $10M / $100M question.
- [ ] In every bottleneck audit, include a second-order pass.
