# Plumbing Before Water

> *"Should we build the plumbing or should we send water through the pipes first? You would build the plumbing first."* — Sam Shvatsa

## The principle

If traffic, systems, and skills are all broken — fix **systems first**. Never push water into pipes that aren't built.

## Why

Two consequences if you reverse the order:

1. **Bad customer experience** — the first leads coming through have a poor experience. Reviews, word-of-mouth, retention all suffer.
2. **Burned marketing dollars** — you pay for leads that don't convert or don't stay.

## How to look at it

Spending on traffic before the system is in place is the most expensive form of learning. You're paying for the lesson that your system doesn't hold — at full price.

## How to apply it

**Pre-flight check before any marketing investment.**

### Pre-flight checklist (system ready?)

- [ ] You know exactly what happens when a lead comes in (within minutes / hours).
- [ ] There is a clear conversion step (booking, form, demo).
- [ ] Conversion rate is measured or roughly known.
- [ ] Onboarding for new customers is documented.
- [ ] Delivery process is reproducible — not just in your head.
- [ ] Capacity check: how many new customers can you actually deliver this month?

If even one is "no" → plumbing first.

## The "smallest viable plumbing" question

> *"What is the smallest version of the plumbing that I can build to get this to work?"*

You don't build the perfect system. You build the **smallest one that holds today** — and iterate.

| Status | Means |
|---|---|
| Smallest viable plumbing | Holds the next 3 months |
| Functional | Holds the next 12 months |
| Scalable | Holds 10x |

Today: smallest viable. In 3 months: re-assess.

## Concrete actions

- [ ] Run the pre-flight checklist on your current setup.
- [ ] Make gaps visible.
- [ ] Before the next marketing initiative, close the most critical gaps.
- [ ] Define "smallest viable" per area in writing.
