# The Two Magic Questions

> *"The two best questions to ask are: What is one thing — if I took away — would not be valuable for you anymore? And what is the one thing you wish I added that would make you stay forever?"* — Sam Shvatsa

## The principle

These two questions replace most market research. They are:
- **Concrete** (one thing, not "your wishes")
- **Hierarchical** (force prioritization)
- **Bidirectional** (what to remove / what to create)

## The two questions verbatim

### Question 1 — Take-away
> *"What is the one thing — if I took it away — would make this collaboration not valuable for you anymore?"*

**What it reveals:** the real core value prop. Not what *you think* makes your service valuable. What is *for the customer* the only anchor.

### Question 2 — Add-on
> *"What is the one thing — if I added it — would make you stay forever?"*

**What it reveals:** the next lever. Where your customer still feels pain or a gap.

## Why "one thing"

If customers can list 5 wishes, you get wishlist noise. When forced to pick *one*, they prioritize — and tell you what actually matters.

## How to look at it

Build your business around the answers to these two questions, not around your own assumptions about what's valuable.

> *"We fear talking to our customers. We think they want all of these things: extra calls, Loom videos, written reports. But they don't care about most of those things."* — Sam Shvatsa

At Telos, all 30 agents were asked. Unanimous answer: *"Being here saves me at least one day per week."* → became the value prop of the entire company.

## How to apply it

**Customer interview protocol:**

1. Contact 10 existing customers (and former ones too!).
2. 20-minute call, framed as: *"I want to make sure we focus our energy on what matters."*
3. Ask both questions.
4. **Don't explain, don't justify** — only listen and follow up.
5. Document answers verbatim (exact words, not paraphrased).
6. After 10 calls: pattern recognition — which answer came up multiple times?

## Follow-up questions (when answers are vague)

- *"Can you tell me more about that?"*
- *"What does that look like day-to-day?"*
- *"When did you last notice this consciously?"*
- *"How much is that worth to you?"*
