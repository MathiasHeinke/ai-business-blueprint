# Skill Stacking — Unrelated Skills Are Your Edge

> *"Tennis, computer science, investment banking, real estate. On paper, none of them go together. And yet none of it went to waste."*

## The principle

You'll rarely be the best in the world at *one* skill. But you can almost certainly be the only person in the world combining **three specific skills** into one person.

Skill stacking creates a position no one can replicate.

## A worked stack

| Skill | What it gave |
|---|---|
| Tennis | Discipline, performance under pressure |
| Computer Science / Math | Reverse engineering, pattern recognition |
| Goldman Sachs | Deal structuring, finance lens |
| Real Estate Operations | Marketing, sales, network, P&L |

The combination = a unique position as Operator + Investor + Strategy + Tech + Sales.

## How to look at it

> *"That random job you're taking to pay bills isn't a waste of time. It's probably teaching you something you'll need one day."*

Skills acquired by chance — out of necessity, through jobs, hobbies — are often *more* valuable than deliberately planned ones, because they come from real life.

## The stacking question

Which 2–3 skills you already have, in combination, are rare?

- *"I can do marketing + I understand bookkeeping + I have design instincts"* → Marketing consulting for creative freelancers who don't have their numbers under control.
- *"I can code + I understand sales + I know logistics industry"* → A vertical tool for logistics companies that automates sales recommendations.

The stack is the answer, not any single skill.

## How to apply it

**Exercise (1 hour, alone):**

1. Run the skill inventory (all skills, honestly).
2. Mark top 5 strong skills.
3. Mark top 3 unusual skills (relative to competitors).
4. Question: which 2–3 of these form a rare combination?
5. Question: where does a market exist for exactly that combination?

## What you gain, what you risk

- **Gain:** Differentiation without 10,000 hours in a single skill
- **Risk:** No one understands what you do if you can't communicate it

→ The stack must be communicable in a value prop (see `../03-customer-discovery/02-one-value-prop-pitch.md`).

## Concrete actions

- [ ] Run the skill inventory.
- [ ] Form 3+ stack hypotheses.
- [ ] Test with 5 people from target markets: *"Would this combination be relevant for you?"*
- [ ] Make the strongest stack your strategic positioning.

## What to avoid

- Pretending to "specialize" in a skill 10,000 others already have
- Stack dilution by stacking too many skills (>4 → unfocused)
